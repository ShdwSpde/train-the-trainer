Culturally Responsive Teaching
CRT connects learning to students' cultural backgrounds, making content more relevant and accessible. It shifts the question from "Can this student learn?" to "How does this student learn best?"

Validate

Affirm students' cultural identities. Acknowledge lived experiences as legitimate knowledge and intellectual assets in the classroom.

Ask students to connect a coding concept to something from their daily life or community.

Center

Use culturally meaningful examples and context. Frame technical problems around real challenges students' communities face.

Build a project that solves a problem in students' neighborhoods — transit apps, local business sites, community boards.

Bridge

Connect classroom learning to career pathways and professional identity. Help students see themselves in the tech industry.

Showcase technologists from similar backgrounds. Discuss how tech skills translate to community impact.
Equity Quick-Wins
Five micro-actions you can start today. These are small moves with outsized impact on classroom belonging and fairness.

Pronounce Names

Ask students how to say their name on day one. Practice until you get it right. Names are identity.

Rotate Cold-Calls

Track who you call on. Use a randomizer or checklist so the same voices don't dominate every session.

Check Participation Bias

After class, review: who spoke? Who didn't? Adjust tomorrow's plan to bring quieter voices in.

Normalize "I Don't Know Yet"

Model uncertainty yourself. Say "I'm not sure — let's find out together" to make not-knowing safe.

Share Your Struggle

Tell students about a time you failed or got stuck learning tech. Vulnerability builds trust and lowers the stakes.

Gradual Release of Responsibility
Move students from dependence to independence using the "I Do, We Do, You Do" framework. Each phase maps to a type of inquiry — use this to scaffold any lesson.
Phase 1 — I Do

Focused Instruction

Teacher models the skill or concept. Students observe and listen. Explain your thinking process out loud.

→
Phase 2 — We Do

Guided Practice

Teacher and students work together. Use think-pair-share, guided coding, and collaborative problem-solving.

→
Phase 3 — You Do

Independent Practice

Students apply learning independently. Circulate, observe, and provide targeted feedback. Resist the urge to re-teach.

Universal Design for Learning
UDL removes barriers by providing multiple means of engagement, representation, and action. Design lessons that work for ALL learners from the start — not as an afterthought.
Engagement

The "Why" of Learning

Provide options for recruiting interest and sustaining motivation.

  • Offer choices in project topics
  • Use real-world problems as hooks
  • Build in collaboration opportunities
  • Provide mastery-oriented feedback
Representation

The "What" of Learning

Present information in multiple formats so all students can access content.

  • Live demo + written tutorial
  • Code alongside visual diagrams
  • Captioned video walkthroughs
  • Highlight patterns across examples
Action & Expression

The "How" of Learning

Give students flexible ways to demonstrate what they know.

  • Code, present, write, or diagram
  • Pair programming as assessment
  • Portfolio over single test
  • Scaffold complex tasks into steps
Socratic Questioning Toolkit
The Socratic method develops critical thinking by guiding students to discover answers themselves. Use these question types to probe deeper understanding instead of simply giving answers.

Clarification

Surface assumptions and ensure understanding
"What do you mean when you say the code 'doesn't work'? What did you expect to happen vs. what actually happened?"

Probing Assumptions

Challenge underlying beliefs about the problem
"What are you assuming about how this function handles null values? What if that assumption is wrong?"

Evidence & Reasoning

Encourage students to support their reasoning
"How do you know this loop runs exactly 5 times? Can you trace through it with me?"

Exploring Viewpoints

Consider alternative approaches
"That's one way to solve it. What would a different approach look like? What are the trade-offs?"

Implications

Think about consequences and connections
"If you change this variable here, what happens to the rest of the program? What could go wrong?"

Meta-Questions

Reflect on the learning process itself
"What strategy did you use to debug this? How would you approach a similar problem next time?"

Types of Student Inquiry
How Deep Should Students Go?

Match your inquiry approach to where your students are in their learning journey.

Most guided

Structured Inquiry

Students follow the lead of the teacher as the entire class engages in one inquiry together.

Teacher-led

Controlled Inquiry

Teacher chooses topics and identifies the resources students will use to answer questions.

Student-directed

Guided Inquiry

Teacher chooses topics/questions and students design product or solution.

Most autonomous

Free Inquiry

Students choose their topics without reference to any prescribed outcome.

Productive Struggle Gauge

In coding/tech classes, struggle is where learning happens — but too much struggle shuts it down. Use these signals to decide.

Let Them Struggle When...

  • ✓ They're trying different approaches and talking it through
  • ✓ The error message gives them enough to debug
  • ✓ Peers are available to collaborate with

Step In When...

  • ✗ They've stopped trying and are just staring at the screen
  • ✗ The blocker is environmental (wrong file, typo, config issue)
  • ✗ Frustration is turning into shutdown or self-doubt
04 — Boundaries
Healthy Boundaries for Educators

Maintaining professional boundaries protects both you and your students. Check with your supervisor for any organization-specific policies.

Physical Boundaries
Physical contact with students is strongly discouraged.
One on one — avoid situations where you are alone with a child.
What about hugs? Do not initiate touch. Attempt a side hug rather than a front-to-front hug.
Verbal Boundaries
No secrets. If a student discloses something that impacts their health and safety, you are required to share that with your supervisor.
Stick to age-appropriate topics.
You don't have to know everything. Redirect, and either commit to finding out together or explain you are not comfortable discussing the topic.
Technological Boundaries
Do not give out your personal email or cell number to students. Use work accounts for program reminders, updates, etc.
Do not accept requests to follow or friend them on social media.
Ideally there is a digital boundaries component to the organizational policies/code of conduct.

QTIP: Quit Taking It Personally!

Behaviors that students display are not because they are actively trying to challenge you. Children are learning and testing what works, and developing social skills to navigate life. Sometimes testing us is part of that. Take a deep breath. It's not personal!

Check Yourself: Boundary Scenarios
Read each situation. Before revealing the answer, decide: is this Appropriate, Needs Caution, or a Boundary Violation?
A student DMs you on Instagram asking for help with a homework problem. You reply with the answer and your phone number "in case they need more help."
What's your call?
Boundary Violation. Never share personal contact info. Redirect to official channels: "Great question! Post that in our Slack channel and I'll answer there."
A student is struggling with a concept. You share a supplemental YouTube tutorial in the class Slack channel and tag the student: "This might help with what you were asking about today!"
What's your call?
Appropriate. You used an official channel, the message is about coursework, and it supports the student's learning. This is exactly what good educators do.
A student tells you they're having a really hard time at home and asks if you can keep it between the two of you. You want to respect their trust.
What's your call?
Needs Caution. Be honest: "I care about you, and if something affects your safety, I may need to share it with someone who can help." No secrets — you're a mandatory reporter.
After class, you and a student bond over a shared interest in gaming. You add them on Discord to continue chatting and play games together on weekends.
What's your call?
Boundary Violation. Personal social connections with students outside the program are off-limits. Channel that energy into classroom activities — build a gaming-themed coding project instead.
A student gives you a big hug at the end of the program's final showcase. Their family is watching and the student is emotional about completing the program.
What's your call?
Needs Caution. You didn't initiate — that's good. Redirect to a side hug, keep it brief, and stay in public view. The student's intent is genuine; your role is to navigate it safely.

05 — Decision Making
The Red, Yellow, Green Framework

This framework outlines three levels of decision-making authority. Think of it like a traffic light for your ideas and changes. It's designed not to restrict you, but to empower you.

Test Your Instincts

Before seeing the full framework, decide: would you need approval for these changes?

You want to swap out a stock photo in your slides for an image that better represents your students' community.
Green! Modifying presentation content to be more culturally responsive is exactly in your lane.
You think the class would benefit from using a new code editor that isn't currently in the tech stack.
Red. New tools must be vetted for security, licensing, and IT support. Submit a Decision Request via Asana.
You want to add a short ungraded warm-up exercise at the start of class to review yesterday's concepts.
Yellow. You can add 1 ungraded exercise per day, but label it clearly as "Optional / Ungraded Practice" in Google Classroom.
Several students are behind, and you want to push tomorrow's graded project deadline back by a week.
Red. Schedule and graded work changes have broad impact. Flag it to your T&L Manager — they may have solutions you haven't considered.
Energy is low after lunch. You want to replace a lecture segment with a think-pair-share breakout activity covering the same material.
Green! Adjusting delivery method and engagement strategies is your superpower. Same content, different vehicle — go for it.

Green Light: Go For It!

Your Agency Zone — Full autonomy, no approval needed

Modify Presentation Content

Swap out images to make them more relevant, culturally responsive, or engaging for your cohort.

Modify Presenter Notes

Adapt notes to fit your personal teaching style, add your own anecdotes or reminders.

Modify Facilitation Guides

Adjust timing or flow of a lesson within the allotted class time to respond to student needs.

Adjust Lesson Delivery

Skip non-essential slides if you've covered a concept well or are short on time.

Enhance Student Engagement

Introduce polls, think-pair-share, breakout rooms, or other interactive elements.

Select Additional Resources

Share relevant articles, videos, tutorials, or blog posts to supplement the core curriculum.

Yellow Light: Proceed with Guardrails

Limited Agency — Minor modifications within pre-defined limits

Adding Ungraded Practice / Exit Tickets

You may add one (1) ungraded exercise per day. Label as "Optional / Ungraded Practice" in Google Classroom.

Modifying Definitions for Key Concepts

Add clarifying examples labeled as "Analogy:" or "Example:". Original definition must remain first.

Adjusting Assignment Format

Add supplementary details within existing sections. Do not change, reorder, or delete primary template headers.

Red Light: Stop and Get Approval

Requires Authorization — Broad impact beyond your classroom

Adding or Modifying Graded Work

All graded work must be standardized to ensure fair and equitable assessment for all students.

Changing Core Learning Objectives

The fundamental skills and outcomes for each module are fixed.

Changes to the AI Use Policy

This is a critical and evolving policy that requires central oversight.

Changes to Course Schedule

Modifying dates, times, or sequence has a significant logistical impact.

Introducing New Tools to the Tech Stack

Any software, platforms, or tools must be approved, vetted for security, and supported by the organization.

Changes to the Brand Kit

Core template (logos, fonts, color schemes) must not be changed to maintain brand consistency.

How to Request Approval

Share an Asana task with the subject "Decision Request: [Topic]". Assign it to your T&L Manager as an "Action Step" in the weekly stand-up. Include: the proposed change, rationale, potential benefit to students, potential risks, and the date you'd like it reviewed by.

Scenario 01

Time Management Crunch

+

The Scenario

You're 30 minutes into a 90-minute JavaScript class. Your planned exercise is taking twice as long as expected.

The Challenge

Several students are still confused. You have two more key concepts to cover before the end of class.

What would you do?
Take a moment to think through your approach before seeing ours.

Suggested Approaches

  • Pause the exercise and do a quick temperature check with the class
  • Prioritize the most critical remaining concept and defer the other to next session
  • Use a "parking lot" — capture unfinished work for follow-up asynchronously
  • Convert the remaining exercise to a guided demo where you code together live
  • Share a recorded walkthrough in Slack for students who need more time
Scenario 02

Supporting Neurodivergent Learners

+

The Scenario

A student consistently struggles to stay focused during long lecture segments. They frequently go off-camera and appear disengaged in breakout rooms.

The Challenge

You want to support this student without singling them out or making assumptions about their needs.

What would you do?
Take a moment to think through your approach before seeing ours.

Suggested Approaches

  • Build in movement breaks and activity transitions every 15-20 minutes for the whole class
  • Offer multiple ways to participate: chat, voice, polls, annotations
  • Check in privately via DM — "How can I best support you in class?"
  • Provide agendas and materials ahead of time so students can prepare
  • Use closed captions and visual aids to support different processing styles
  • Normalize cameras-off breaks — "Take 2 minutes, stretch, come back ready"
Scenario 03

Addressing Knowledge Gaps

+

The Scenario

You're teaching arrays in JavaScript, but half the class is still struggling with variables and basic syntax from the previous module.

The Challenge

Moving forward leaves students behind; going back bores those who are ready. You need to serve both groups.

What would you do?
Take a moment to think through your approach before seeing ours.

Suggested Approaches

  • Use a quick diagnostic poll or exit ticket to gauge where everyone is
  • Split into differentiated breakout rooms: review group and advanced group
  • Pair struggling students with stronger peers for collaborative exercises
  • Provide a "Level Up" challenge for advanced students while you review basics
  • Share supplemental review resources in Slack for independent practice
  • Flag to your T&L Manager if the gap is systemic across the cohort
Scenario 04

Low Energy / Silent Classroom

+

The Scenario

It's the second week and your class has gone completely silent. Nobody answers questions, cameras are off, and chat is empty.

The Challenge

You need to rebuild engagement without making it feel forced or punitive.

What would you do?
Take a moment to think through your approach before seeing ours.

Suggested Approaches

  • Start with a low-stakes warm-up: "Drop your favorite song in chat" or a poll
  • Use the Waterfall technique — everyone types but doesn't send until you say "go"
  • Acknowledge the silence without shaming: "I notice it's quiet — that's okay, let's try something different"
  • Switch to smaller breakout rooms (2-3 people) where students feel safer talking
  • Give private shout-outs via DM to students who participate to build confidence
  • Consider whether the content/pacing needs adjusting based on energy levels
Co-facilitation Signals
When you're co-teaching or have a TA, use these signals in the Zoom chat or via DM to stay in sync without interrupting the flow.
✋

I Have Something to Add

DM your co-facilitator: "Can I jump in after this point?" Avoids talking over each other.

⏲

Time Check

DM: "10 min left" or "We're over time." Keep each other on pace without public interruptions.

👋

I'll Take This

DM: "I'll field this question." Prevents both facilitators from jumping on the same student question.

🚨

Student Needs Help

DM: "Check on [name] in breakout 3." The co-facilitator handles it while you keep teaching.

Waterfall in Chat

Everyone types their answer but doesn't hit send until you say "Go!" — then a waterfall of responses floods the chat simultaneously.

How to Run

  1. Pose a question or prompt to the class
  2. Say "Type your answer in chat but DON'T hit enter yet"
  3. Wait 15-20 seconds for everyone to type
  4. Count down "3, 2, 1 — GO!" and everyone sends at once
  5. Read through responses aloud, highlight interesting answers

"I noticed you..." / "That was strong because..." / "You really leveled up on..."

Redirect

"One thing to try next time..." / "What if you approached it by..." / "Let's revisit this part..."

Push

"What would happen if..." / "How could you extend this to..." / "Can you defend that choice?"

Slack & Async Norms

  • Slack messages

    Respond within 4 hours on weekdays. No expectation on evenings/weekends.

  • Async assignments

    Return feedback within 24 hours. If you need more time, acknowledge receipt.

  • Channels vs DMs

    Default to channels for transparency. Use DMs only for private/sensitive topics.

  • Office hours

    Post recurring availability in your cohort channel. Use Zoom for drop-in sessions.

Exit Ticket Templates

Comprehension Check

"Explain [today's concept] in your own words in 2-3 sentences."

Muddiest Point

"What's one thing from today that still feels unclear or confusing?"

Confidence Poll

"Rate 1-5: How confident are you that you could teach today's topic to a friend?"

One Thing

"Name one thing you'll do differently in your next project based on today."
